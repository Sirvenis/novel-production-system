# Chapter 1: The Regular Customer

**Job Statement:** Establish Di's ordinary world, her competence, and the first wrongness — all in a single supermarket interaction.

**Word target:** 3,000–3,500

---

Mrs. Patterson buys the same thing every Tuesday.

Two litres of milk, one loaf of sliced white, one packet of custard creams. She has been buying the same thing every Tuesday since before Di started at Tesco, which was fifteen years ago. Di does not need to look at the receipt to know what Mrs. Patterson has bought. She does not need to look at Mrs. Patterson to know she is about to complain about the price of cheese.

What she needs to look at is Mrs. Patterson's face.

Because Mrs. Patterson is looking at Di like she has never seen her before.

---

The checkout beeps. Di swipes the milk, the bread, the custard creams. She has done this thousands of times. The barcode scanner knows her hand better than Mick ever did, and Mick knew her hand well enough to take off a wedding ring without asking.

"That'll be three forty-two, Mrs. Patterson."

Mrs. Patterson does not reach for her purse. She is still looking at Di. Her head is tilted slightly, the way people tilt their heads when they are trying to place a face — a neighbour's daughter, a woman from the doctor's, someone they used to know and cannot quite name.

"You're new here, aren't you?"

Di's thumb freezes over the receipt printer. She has been on checkout three since 2011. She has rung up Mrs. Patterson's two litres of milk and her custard creams every Tuesday for fifteen years, except for the six Tuesdays in 2019 when Mrs. Patterson had her hip done and her daughter did the shopping. Di sent flowers. She put twenty quid in the envelope because that was what you did.

"Not especially new, Mrs. Patterson. Same face, same checkout."

Mrs. Patterson laughs. It is a small laugh, polite, the laugh of someone humouring a stranger. She opens her purse — the same red purse she has had since Di can remember, the one with the clasp that sticks — and counts out three pound coins and forty-two pence in exact change. She has never once given Di a twenty and waited for change. Mrs. Patterson believes in exactness.

"Well," she says, folding her receipt with the precision of a woman who files everything. "You do a lovely job, dear. Much better than the last girl."

She picks up her bag and walks toward the exit, moving slowly because of the hip. The automatic doors open for her. The automatic doors have opened for Mrs. Patterson two thousand times, and they have opened for Di every morning at six for fifteen years.

Di watches her go. The receipt in her hand is still warm.

---

"What?"

Sharon is staring at her. Sharon has been staring at her since Mrs. Patterson said the thing about the last girl. Sharon is on checkout one, which means she can see Di and also see the doors and also see most of the store, because Sharon has the neck of a barn owl and the attention span of a goldfish with grievances.

"Nothing," Di says.

"Your face said something."

"My face says a lot of things, Sharon. Most of them aren't polite."

Sharon snorts. She is forty-seven and has worked at this Tesco since it opened in 2008. She was here before the self-service machines, before the click-and-collect, before management started sending motivational posters about being brand ambassadors. Sharon remembers when the staff room had a proper kettle and not the thing that makes coffee taste like burnt plastic.

"Old bat forget her own head if it weren't screwed on," Sharon says.

"She's not an old bat. She's eighty-three and she remembers the price of everything."

"Then she's got selective memory, because she's been talking to you since God were a lad."

Di says nothing. She scans the next customer's basket — a young man in a tracksuit buying energy drinks and frozen pizzas — and tries to remember the last time she saw Mrs. Patterson look at her like that. She cannot remember. Mrs. Patterson has never looked at her like that.

The young man pays with his phone. Di hates phone payments. They always take twice as long because the customer holds the phone at the wrong angle and the scanner beeps and the customer looks at her like it is her fault the technology does not work. This one works on the third try.

"Cheers, love," the young man says, which is what they all say, which is what they have always said, which means nothing.

---

The morning shift ends at two. Di hands over to Marcus, who is twenty-two and studying criminology at Sheffield Hallam, which Di thinks means he watches too much American television and has opinions about police procedure. Marcus is a good worker. He shows up on time, he does not steal from the till, and he only talks about his degree when someone asks. Di appreciates all three qualities.

"All right, Marcus?"

"Sound, Di. How's it been?"

"Usual. Price complaints, dodgy tomatoes, someone crying in aisle four."

"That was me," says a voice from behind the bakery counter. Davo. Davo is fifty-one and has worked in the bakery since before it was a bakery, when this corner of the store sold electrical goods and Davo sold them. Now he sells bread and pretends he does not miss the radios.

"What were you crying about?" Sharon calls from checkout one.

"Management," Davo says. "Same as always."

Management is a single entity at this Tesco. It does not have a face or a name. It sends emails about upselling and customer engagement and occasionally it visits in a suit and stands near the fridges with a tablet, looking at numbers. Di has learned to spot a management visit by the sudden appearance of someone in a blazer who does not know where the toilet cleaner is.

"They want me to do a display for the bakery," Davo says. "A 'theatrical experience.' Their words."

"What kind of theatrical experience can you do with sliced white?" Sharon asks.

"That's what I said. They showed me a picture of a Tesco in Leeds where they built a castle out of baguettes."

Di laughs despite herself. The laugh feels strange, like it belongs to someone else. She is still thinking about Mrs. Patterson's face.

"You all right?" Marcus asks quietly.

"Fine. Just tired."

"You look like you've seen a ghost."

Di looks at him. Marcus has kind eyes. He is the sort of young man who will make a good father if he does not watch too much television first.

"I've seen worse than ghosts, Marcus. Ghosts don't ask for a refund."

He smiles and takes the till drawer. Di goes to the canteen.

---

The canteen is a room the size of a large garage, painted in a colour that was probably meant to be calming but has faded to the shade of old gravy. There are six tables, twenty-four plastic chairs, a microwave that works on Tuesdays, and a noticeboard with the same health and safety poster that has been there since 2014. The poster shows a man lifting a box incorrectly. Di has looked at this man lifting a box incorrectly possibly ten thousand times. She no longer sees him.

She sits at the table by the window. The window looks out onto the car park, which is mostly empty at this hour. Beyond the car park there is a fence, and beyond the fence there is a field that used to belong to the National Coal Board, and beyond the field there is the moor, which is grey and brown and has been there since before the town, before the pit, before Tesco, before Di.

The rain has been there since October. She cannot remember what dry feels like.

Sharon comes in with two cups of tea. She puts one in front of Di and sits down without asking. Sharon never asks. Sharon operates on the principle that if you are sitting in the canteen, you want company, and if you do not want company, you should have gone somewhere else.

"So," Sharon says. "What did she actually say?"

"Nothing. She forgot my face. It happens."

"It doesn't happen. Mrs. Patterson knows everyone's face. She knows my face, and I only see her when she's buying your custard creams."

"She didn't forget my face. She just... looked at me like she was placing me."

"Placing you?"

"You know. Like when you see someone and you can't work out where from."

Sharon sips her tea. Sharon drinks tea in the way Di's father drank beer — steadily, without hurry, as if it were a job that needed doing right.

"That's funny," Sharon says.

"What's funny?"

"Mrs. Patterson told me yesterday she likes the new girl on checkout three. Said she's much better than the previous one. Politer. More efficient."

Di's tea is halfway to her mouth. She puts it down.

"There isn't a new girl on checkout three."

"I know."

"There hasn't been a new girl on checkout three in six months. Since Chantelle left."

"I know, Di."

"I'm checkout three."

"I know you are."

They look at each other. The microwave ticks. Outside, the rain makes a sound against the window that Di has stopped hearing because she has heard it every day for fifteen years.

"She's old," Sharon says finally. "Old people get confused."

"Mrs. Patterson isn't confused. She remembered the price of cheese."

"Maybe she meant checkout one. Maybe there's a new girl on checkout one I haven't noticed."

Sharon is on checkout one. Sharon would notice if someone moved a crisp display two inches to the left. Sharon would notice a new girl on checkout one while asleep.

"Maybe," Di says.

They drink their tea. The rain continues. The man on the poster continues to lift his box incorrectly.

---

Di leaves at half past two. She walks through the car park, past the empty spaces where the morning shoppers have gone home, past the fence, past the field. She lives on Mill Lane, which is a street of terraced houses built in 1897 for pit workers. The pit closed in 1994. The houses are still here, though most of them have been sold and sold again and now belong to people who work at the call centre on the industrial estate or who commute to Sheffield.

Di rents number 14. It is small. The front door sticks in winter. The back garden is concrete because the previous owner got tired of mowing. There is a shed that Mick built in 2012, the year before the divorce, and the shed is slowly becoming one with the garden fence. Di does not go in the shed.

Bitey is at the window when she gets home. Bitey is a terrier cross of uncertain parentage who is now fourteen and too old to bark. He communicates through a system of sighs and meaningful looks. When Di opens the door, he sighs in a way that means he is glad she is home and also disappointed in her for taking so long.

"Don't start," Di says.

She feeds him. She makes a sandwich — cheese and pickle, the same thing she has had for lunch every working day for fifteen years. She sits at the kitchen table, which is Formica and was here when she moved in, and she eats the sandwich and tries not to think about Mrs. Patterson's face.

It does not work.

Mrs. Patterson has known Di since Di was nineteen. Mrs. Patterson watched Di get married, though she did not attend the wedding because she does not hold with registry offices. Mrs. Patterson sent a card when Di's mother died, a card with a picture of lilies and handwriting so perfect it looked printed. Mrs. Patterson knows that Di drinks her tea strong and her coffee weak and that she does not eat biscuits because of her teeth.

Mrs. Patterson has never once looked at Di like she was a stranger.

Di finishes her sandwich. Bitey sighs again, this time from his bed in the corner, and Di realises she has been sitting at the table for twenty minutes without moving.

"Fuck," she says to the empty kitchen. "Fuck, fuck, fuck."

Bitey does not react. He has heard worse.

---

Wednesday is Di's day off. She does laundry. She walks Bitey to the field behind the Tesco, which is not her favourite walk because it smells of the industrial estate, but Bitey likes the rabbits. She watches television — a programme about people buying houses in Spain, which she finds both depressing and incomprehensible. She does not think about Mrs. Patterson for almost four hours.

At half past six, her phone rings. It is her sister, Linda, who lives in Chesterfield and works in insurance and calls every Wednesday at half past six because Linda believes in routines the way other people believe in God.

"How are you?" Linda asks.

"Fine."

"You don't sound fine."

"I sound like I always sound."

"You sound tired."

"I'm always tired, Linda. I'm a shift supervisor at Tesco."

Linda makes a noise that is not quite sympathy and not quite judgment. It is a noise she has been making since they were children, since their father worked at the pit and their mother worked at the Co-op and Linda decided early that she would not end up in either place. She did not end up in either place. She ended up in insurance in Chesterfield, which is not the same thing as triumph but is not the same thing as failure either.

"Mick's got a new van," Linda says. "Jen's name on the side."

"Good for them."

"You don't mean that."

"I mean it for them. I don't have to mean it for me."

There is a pause. Di can hear Linda's television in the background — the same programme about Spanish houses, playing ten minutes behind.

"Are you eating properly?"

"I had cheese and pickle for lunch."

"That's not eating properly."

"It's eating, Linda. That's the properly part."

Linda laughs despite herself. Di has always been able to make Linda laugh. It was the one thing they had in common as children, the one thing that survived their father's death and their mother's decline and Linda's marriage to a man who sold double glazing and talked about golf. The laughter. The ability to find something funny in the middle of everything else.

"I worry about you," Linda says.

"I know."

"On your own in that house."

"I'm not on my own. I've got Bitey."

"Bitey's fourteen."

"He's got years left."

"He's got arthritis, Di."

Di looks at Bitey, who is asleep in his bed, dreaming of rabbits. His legs twitch. He makes a small sound that is not quite a bark because he does not have the energy for barking anymore.

"We all have arthritis, Linda. It's the human condition."

"You're not going to take this seriously, are you?"

"I'm going to take it exactly as seriously as it deserves."

Another pause. The television says something about coastal views.

"I love you," Linda says.

"I love you too. Stop worrying."

"I'll stop worrying when you stop giving me reasons."

Di hangs up and looks at Bitey and thinks about Mrs. Patterson and thinks about Mick's van with Jen's name on the side and thinks about the man on the poster lifting his box incorrectly and thinks about nothing at all for a while.

---

Thursday morning. Di arrives at Tesco at five to six. The store is quiet, the lights still on their morning setting, bright and unforgiving. Marcus is on checkout one, looking bleary. Davo is in the bakery, arranging sliced white in a way that is not theatrical. Sharon is in the canteen, drinking tea and reading a magazine about celebrity weddings.

"You're early," Sharon says.

"Couldn't sleep."

"The thing?"

"What thing?"

"The Mrs. Patterson thing."

Di shrugs. She makes a cup of tea — strong, two sugars — and sits down. The rain has paused, which means it will start again in approximately twenty minutes. The field behind the fence is grey-green and still.

"I sorted it out," Di says.

"Sorted what out?"

"The thing. Mrs. Patterson got confused. Old people do that. It's sorted."

Sharon looks at her for a long moment. Sharon has known Di for twelve years. Sharon was at Di's mother's funeral. Sharon brought a quiche.

"If you say so, love."

"I say so."

Di drinks her tea. The clock on the wall says six oh three. The store opens at seven. The first customers will arrive at ten past, the early pensioners who like to beat the rush, who buy their milk before the world wakes up.

Mrs. Patterson is not an early pensioner. Mrs. Patterson arrives at half past ten, every Tuesday, for fifteen years. Mrs. Patterson does not shop on Thursdays.

Mrs. Patterson walks through the automatic doors at ten past eleven.

Di sees her from the canteen window. She sees the red purse, the familiar coat, the careful walk that accommodates the hip. She sees Mrs. Patterson pause inside the doors and look around the store the way she always does — assessing the layout, noting any changes, preparing her route.

Then Mrs. Patterson looks up at the canteen window.

She looks directly at Di.

And she smiles.

It is a polite smile, a stranger's smile, the smile of someone who has seen a face in a window and is acknowledging its existence without claiming any knowledge of it. It is the smile you give to someone you do not know but do not wish to offend.

Di puts down her tea.

Mrs. Patterson turns and walks toward the bakery. She buys two litres of milk, one loaf of sliced white, one packet of custard creams. She pays at checkout three.

Di is not on checkout three. Marcus is on checkout three. Di is still in the canteen, still holding her cup, still looking at the space where Mrs. Patterson stood.

Marcus scans the milk, the bread, the custard creams. Mrs. Patterson counts out exact change.

"You do such a lovely job, dear," Mrs. Patterson says. "Much better than the last girl."

Di is the last girl.

Marcus says thank you. Mrs. Patterson picks up her bag and walks toward the exit. The automatic doors open for her. The automatic doors have opened for Mrs. Patterson two thousand and one times, and they have never once opened for someone who did not exist.

Di puts the cup down. It is cold.

She looks at Sharon. Sharon is looking at Mrs. Patterson's back, at the red purse, at the careful walk.

"Di," Sharon says.

"Don't."

"Di, she —"

"I said don't."

Di stands up. She walks to the window. The rain has started again, a fine persistent rain that does not stop so much as continue. Mrs. Patterson is at the bus stop now, waiting for the 47, the same bus she has taken for fifteen years.

Di is nineteen again, ringing up her first customer, learning the barcode scanner, dropping a pound coin and watching it roll under the till. She is twenty-two, wearing her mother's earrings, getting married at the registry office while her father sits in the front row and does not cry because he would rather choke than do it in public. She is thirty-one, signing divorce papers in a solicitor's office in Sheffield, listening to Mick say "it's not working" in a voice that suggests he has rehearsed it. She is thirty-four, standing in a canteen window, watching an old woman wait for a bus she has taken two thousand times.

The old woman does not know her.

The old woman has never known her.

Or — worse — the old woman knows someone else who is better at being Di than Di is.

Di turns away from the window. She goes to the staff toilet and throws up her tea.

When she comes out, Sharon is waiting in the corridor. Sharon looks frightened, which is worse than anything, because Sharon has seen everything — the Christmas rush riots, the time someone set fire to the recycling bin, the year a customer died in aisle four and Davo had to move the body before the children saw.

"You all right?" Sharon asks.

"Fine," Di says.

"You don't look fine."

"What do I look like?"

Sharon hesitates. Then she says, "You look like someone who's just been replaced."

Di laughs. It is not a good laugh. It is a laugh that belongs in a different kind of story, the kind where someone has a breakdown in a supermarket and the manager calls an ambulance and everyone stands around feeling relieved it is not them.

"Replaced," Di says. "By who?"

Sharon does not answer. She does not have to. They both know there is no new girl on checkout three. They both know there is no replacement waiting in the wings. They both know that Mrs. Patterson is old and forgetful and probably confused and probably thinking of someone else and probably —

Probably —

Di goes back to work. She finishes her shift. She does not think about Mrs. Patterson again until Friday morning, when she wakes up at four and cannot remember her own face in the mirror.

The person looking back at her is not quite right.

She looks better.

---

**End of Chapter 1**
