# Chapter Rewrite from Scratch: When a Draft Is Rejected

## Context

Book 2, Chapter 4. First draft received C+ from editorial (BLOCKED) and B+/A- from reader. The draft conflated three architecture chapters into one. Decision: rewrite from scratch rather than patch.

## The Decision Framework

When a chapter receives a BLOCKED or C-grade verdict:

| Option | When to Use | Trade-off |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| **Patch in-place** | Minor fixes (one scene, a few lines, a POV break) | Fast, preserves good material, low risk |
| **Rewrite from scratch** | Structural failure (architecture conflation, wrong mandate, scope creep) | Time-intensive but produces cleaner result; allows re-imagining beats |
| **Revise architecture** | The architecture itself is wrong, not the draft | Big decision; affects downstream chapters; user must approve |

**Rule:** If the problem is "the draft tried to do too much," rewrite from scratch with strict constraints. If the problem is "the draft didn't do enough of the right thing," patch in-place.

## The Rewrite Process

### Step 1: Lock the Preserved Beats

Both editorial and reader identified what must survive. Write these down as sacred:

- Lena's water-quality interrogation of Jim Patterson
- The flow-rate anomaly (23% increased north, decreased south)
- "This is not a pest. This is an infrastructure."
- Margaret's tea / Jack's wrench-noise domestic texture
- Jim Patterson's grandfather: "The old mill used to sing at night"

### Step 2: Strip Everything That Doesn't Belong

Identify what violated the mandate and remove it completely:

- Raj's full frequency reveal → reduce to setup tease only
- Old Bill's phone call → remove entirely (save for Ch 6)
- Dr. Thorne's video call → remove entirely (save for Ch 6)
- Maya's full scene → remove entirely (save for Ch 6)
- Crew assembly montage → remove entirely (save for Ch 6)

### Step 3: Add What the Mandate Requires but Was Missing

The editorial identified gaps:

- Physical investigation beats (not just maps and phone calls)
- Nate and Lena at a junction channel, seeing the fungal growth
- A farmer (Schmidt) who represents the human-scale denial
- An animal witness (the dog) that senses what humans refuse to

### Step 4: Draft with the Mandate Written at the Top

Before writing a single paragraph, write the architecture mandate as a header comment. Refer to it constantly. If a scene doesn't serve that mandate, it doesn't belong.

### Step 5: Run the Pipeline on the Rewrite

Do not self-approve. Deploy editorial and reader profiles against the new draft. If it passes, commit. If it doesn't, iterate.

## Lessons

1. **Rewriting from scratch is faster than patching a fundamentally wrong draft.** Three hours of patchwork vs. one hour of clean drafting.
2. **Preserved beats are anchors.** Knowing what must survive makes the rewrite feel less like destruction and more like relocation.
3. **The editorial review of the rewrite should reference the first review.** The editor should explicitly compare: "This version fixes the architecture conflation from the first draft. It now stays within its mandate."
4. **Word count often drops in the rewrite.** The first draft was bloated with stolen beats. The rewrite is leaner. This is correct.
5. **Post-review micro-fixes are normal.** Even a B+ draft needs 2-3 small fixes after review. Apply them before committing.
