# Scope Discipline for Series Expansion

**When:** After completing Book N and before architecting Book N+1. Also when evaluating whether a sequel hook should become a primary plot thread or remain a seed.

**Purpose:** Prevent a focused horror novel from becoming a "crowded mythology exchange" by enforcing single-frontier discipline per book.

---

## The User's Directive (Verbatim)

> "The Simpson Desert thread is already strong enough to carry the next novel. The Baltic Sea should remain a distant pressure point unless Book 4 genuinely needs it. Two frontiers at once could turn a focused horror novel into a crowded mythology exchange."

> "My direction is: Preserve Book 3. Update canon. Architect Book 4 around Simpson Desert 0.47 Hz. Keep the Baltic Sea as a secondary series seed."

**Core principle:** One book = one frontier. Secondary seeds remain distant pressure points, not parallel storylines.

---

## The Single-Frontier Rule

Each book in the series should have **exactly one primary frontier** — the location and entity the crew directly confronts. All other threads are:
- **Referenced** (mentioned in dialogue, phone calls, emails)
- **Worried about** (characters express concern but do not act)
- **Seeded for future books** (plant now, pay off two books later)

**What a secondary seed IS in the current book:**
- Evidence that the Pattern is bigger than the current location
- Pressure on the protagonist to feel the weight of a global threat
- A reason for supporting characters (Toby, Raj) to expand their networks
- A source of anxiety that drives character decisions

**What a secondary seed is NOT:**
- A parallel storyline with its own POV chapters
- A location the crew visits or investigates
- A new entity they confront directly
- A split narrative where half the crew goes to Location A and half to Location B

## The "Distant Pressure Point" Pattern

When a sequel hook is strong but should not become the primary plot:

| Mechanic | Example (Book 3 → Book 4) |
|----------|---------------------------|
| Phone call | Toby calls Nate from Birmingham about the Baltic Sea |
| Blog post | Toby's blog documents the German student's report |
| Email | Raj forwards a sensor reading from the Baltic to the crew |
| Conversation | Nate worries aloud: "If it's happening there, how many places is it happening?" |
| Monitoring | Raj's expanded network picks up distant frequencies — noted but not investigated |

**Key constraint:** The Baltic Sea is NEVER visited in Book 4. The crew NEVER meets the German student. The threat is REAL but DISTANT.

## Anti-Patterns (What to Avoid)

### Anti-Pattern 1: The Split Crew
"Half the crew goes to the Simpson Desert, half to the Baltic Sea, and they coordinate via satellite phone."

**Why it fails:** Splits narrative focus, dilutes tension, makes both frontiers feel thin. Horror needs claustrophobia and intimacy. Splitting the crew destroys both.

### Anti-Pattern 2: The Parallel Entity
"The Simpson Desert entity and the Baltic Sea entity are revealed to be the same intelligence, and the crew must defeat them both simultaneously."

**Why it fails:** Turns a grounded horror novel into a global conspiracy thriller. The Pattern is NOT coordinated. The entities are independent. Connecting them prematurely collapses the mystery into explanation.

### Anti-Pattern 3: The Seed Consumption
"Since we're introducing the Baltic Sea anyway, let's make it Book 4's B-plot."

**Why it fails:** Consumes a seed that was meant for Book 5 or 6. The series loses long-term runway. Each book should plant MORE seeds than it consumes.

### Anti-Pattern 4: The "Let's Visit" Temptation
"The crew has to go to Germany for a conference anyway, so they investigate the Baltic while they're there."

**Why it fails:** Contrived travel violates the working-class pragmatism of the series. The crew is not international investigators. They are pest control technicians who happen to be in Queensland.

## Scope Discipline Checklist (Before Architecting Book N+1)

- [ ] **Primary frontier identified:** One location, one entity, one threat
- [ ] **Secondary seeds listed:** All sequel hooks from previous books
- [ ] **Secondary seed roles assigned:** For each, determine if it is REFERENCED, WORRIED ABOUT, or SEEDED FOR LATER
- [ ] **No parallel storylines:** Confirm the crew does not split
- [ ] **No new global conspiracy:** Confirm entities remain independent
- [ ] **More seeds planted than consumed:** Book N+1 should plant at least 2 new seeds for future books
- [ ] **User approval obtained:** Present the scope summary and confirm single-frontier discipline

## When to Break the Rule

The single-frontier rule can be broken only when:
1. **The user explicitly requests it** — "I want Book 5 to have two frontiers"
2. **A secondary seed has grown too strong to ignore** — if the Baltic Sea develops its own 0.23 Hz fundamental frequency that threatens to eclipse the Simpson Desert, reassess
3. **The series structure demands it** — Book 8+ may need a "Pattern convergence" book where multiple threads collide

**Even then:** Break it in a LATE book (6+), not an early one. Early books need focus to establish the series engine.
