### Voice Risk Flag

**Flagged passage:** Lines 329–330 (flashback montage)

> "Di is nineteen again, ringing up her first customer... She is twenty-two, wearing her mother's earrings... She is thirty-one, signing divorce papers... She is thirty-four, standing in a canteen window..."

This is the most stylistically elevated passage in the chapter. The parallel structure is rhythmic and deliberate — more poetic than Di's usual prose. However:
- It is concrete, not abstract
- Each image is specific (barcode scanner, mother's earrings, divorce papers, bus stop)
- It serves an emotional function: Di's life collapsing into a single moment of not-being-known
- It appears at the climax of the chapter, where elevated prose is earned

**Verdict:** Borderline but acceptable. The guardrail says Di should not use "abstract philosophical language." This is not abstract. It is compressed memory. If Arden disagrees, it can be rewritten as separate sentences without the parallel structure. No action required unless flagged by reader.

## 4. Does the Better Version Concept Survive First Contact with Prose?

**Yes.**

The replacement horror works on the page because:

1. **The wrongness is small and deniable.** Mrs. Patterson does not scream. She smiles politely. The horror is in the gap between what should happen and what does.
2. **The confirmation comes from a second witness.** Sharon's independent report ("new girl on checkout three") makes the wrongness real, not subjective.
3. **The escalation is patient.** Tuesday → canteen confirmation → Thursday visit → final mirror scene. The dread builds through accumulation, not revelation.
4. **The replacement is not seen.** There is no monster, no clone, no confrontation. Only absence and misrecognition. This is the concept's strength.
5. **The final line lands.** "The person looking back at her is not quite right. She looks better." This is the core wound in nine words.

**Arden reader-experience check:**
- **Laugh in first chapter?** Yes — baguette castle, management as single entity, goldfish with grievances, "Ghosts don't ask for a refund."
- **Frown in third chapter?** Not applicable — this is Ch. 1 review. But the frown starts here: Mrs. Patterson's smile.
- **Concept clarity?** The reader understands exactly what is wrong without understanding why. Perfect.

## 5. Does South Yorkshire Remain the Correct Setting?

**Yes.**

The setting is doing thematic work the other candidates could not do as cleanly:

- **The landscape already knows replacement.** The closed pit, the empty mill, the high street with more bookies than shops — this is a place that has already been optimised. The replacement horror is not foreign. It is the next iteration of a process the town has already survived.
- **The rain is a character.** "The rain has been there since October. She cannot remember what dry feels like." This is not weather description. It is emotional state. The persistence of rain mirrors the persistence of dread.
- **The supermarket is social infrastructure.** Tesco is not just a workplace. It is the town's meeting place, its memory, its ritual. Mrs. Patterson's Tuesday visit is a sacrament. When the sacrament fails, the town's coherence fails.
- **The voice fits.** Northern English understatement — the refusal to name emotion directly — is the perfect vehicle for horror that operates through politeness and omission.

**Ireland would also work**, but it would be a different book. The Irish Midlands voice is more musical, more verbally inventive, more inclined to irony and storytelling. The Better Version requires dryness, restraint, and exhaustion. Yorkshire delivers that more naturally.
