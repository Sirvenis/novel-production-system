# Chapter Job Statement

## What It Is

A single sentence that declares what a chapter must make the reader believe or feel. It is the chapter's reason for existing.

## Format

> This chapter exists to make the reader believe [X].

Or:

> This chapter exists to make the reader feel [X].

## Examples

- "This chapter exists to make the reader believe the entity can think."
- "This chapter exists to make the reader feel the protagonist's isolation before the reunion."
- "This chapter exists to make the reader believe the crew's strategy will fail."

## Rules

1. One sentence only.
2. Focus on reader effect, not plot summary.
3. "Believe" is for revelations, discoveries, and realisations.
4. "Feel" is for emotional beats, relationships, and atmosphere.
5. The Job Statement goes at the top of every chapter draft as a constraint.
6. If the draft exceeds its Job Statement, stop and split.

## Anti-Patterns

- ❌ "This chapter is about the protagonist going to the store." (Plot summary, not reader effect)
- ❌ "This chapter introduces the supporting cast." (Mechanical, not emotional)
- ❌ "This chapter is 4,000 words of action." (Word count is not a job)

## When to Revisit

If during drafting you discover the chapter's actual job is different from the architecture:
1. Update the Job Statement in the draft
2. Flag it in the tracker
3. Check whether this changes downstream chapters
4. If yes, update the architecture before proceeding
