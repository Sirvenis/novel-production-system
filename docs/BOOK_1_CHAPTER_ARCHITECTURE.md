## Act Structure Overview

| Act | Chapters | Content | Word Target | Tone |
|-----|----------|---------|-------------|------|
| 1 | 1–4 | Ordinary world. Di's routine. First wrongnesses. | 12,000 | Competent, dry, grounded |
| 2 | 5–10 | Investigation. Discovery of replacement. Attempts to compete. | 22,000 | Methodical, increasingly desperate |
| 3 | 11–14 | Acceptance. The replacement takes over. Di's final days as herself. | 14,000 | Bitter, observational, hollow |

## Chapter Job Statements

### ACT 1: THE ORDINARY WORLD

#### Chapter 1: The Regular Customer
**Word target:** 3,500 (actual: 3,613 ✓)
**Job Statement:** This chapter exists to make the reader believe Di is a real, competent person before showing the first crack.
**Key beats:**
- Di's competence established through routine (Mrs. Patterson, exact change, barcode scanner)
- Workplace ensemble introduced (Sharon, Marcus, Davo) with economic efficiency
- Di's home life: Mill Lane terrace, Bitey, cheese and pickle, Linda phone call
- First wrongness: Mrs. Patterson does not recognise Di; reports a "new girl" on checkout three
- Final line: Di sees herself in mirror, looks better
**Emotional state:** Competent, tired, dryly humorous — disturbed but not yet alarmed
**Explained:** Nothing. Only observed wrongness.
**Withheld:** Everything. Origin, mechanism, scale.
**Hinted:** The wrongness is small, social, deniable.
**Status:** Drafted and approved.

---

#### Chapter 2: The New Girl on Checkout Three
**Word target:** 3,000
**Job Statement:** This chapter exists to make the reader feel the wrongness expand beyond one customer into Di's daily life.
**Key beats:**
- Di tries to verify Mrs. Patterson's confusion: asks Sharon, checks staff rosters, finds no "new girl"
- Second wrongness: young customer thanks Di by name — but uses a name Di never told him
- Third wrongness: Di's locker combination has been changed; her apron is folded differently than she left it
- Sharon reports seeing "someone who looked like Di" on checkout three yesterday afternoon — when Di was at home with Bitey
- Di realises the wrongness is not memory but presence: someone else is doing her job when she is not there
**Emotional state:** Alarmed, methodical, beginning to track
**Explained:** The replacement is not a delusion — it has physical traces.
**Withheld:** Who the replacement is, where it comes from, what it wants.
**Hinted:** The replacement is better at the job than Di. The manager compliments "Di's" performance on a shift she did not work.

---

#### Chapter 3: The Thing That Doesn't Add Up
**Word target:** 3,000
**Job Statement:** This chapter exists to make the reader believe Di will try to investigate rather than dismiss.
**Key beats:**
- Di creates a private log: dates, times, witnesses, discrepancies
- She tests the wrongness: deliberately misshelves an item, notes the correction next day
- She asks Marcus to confirm he saw her on a specific shift; Marcus is confused — he did see her, but she was "different somehow"
- Linda calls; Di almost tells her, then doesn't — can't say it aloud without sounding mad
- Di realises she has no proof that would convince anyone else; the wrongness is designed to be invisible to outsiders
**Emotional state:** Methodical, isolated, beginning to doubt her own perception
**Explained:** Di's investigative method and her competence at tracking.
**Withheld:** Whether the replacement is supernatural, technological, or psychological.
**Hinted:** The replacement leaves no evidence that would survive scrutiny. It is optimised for deniability.

---

#### Chapter 4: The Photograph
**Word target:** 2,500
**Job Statement:** This chapter exists to make the reader believe there is objective evidence of replacement.
**Key beats:**
- CCTV footage exists but management refuses to show it without a "formal incident report"
- Di finds a photograph on her phone she does not remember taking: herself at the store, wearing a different coat, smiling at a customer she does not recognise
- The timestamp says Tuesday afternoon. Di was at home with Bitey on Tuesday afternoon.
- Di studies the photo: the smile is hers but not hers — wider, more engaged, more patient
- She deletes the photo, then recovers it from Recently Deleted. She cannot stop looking at it.
- Act 1 closes with Di understanding: this is not confusion. This is invasion.
**Emotional state:** Horrified, strangely fascinated, professionally violated
**Explained:** The replacement exists in physical space and leaves physical traces.
**Withheld:** How the photo was taken, whether the replacement knows it exists.
**Hinted:** The replacement is not hiding. It does not think it needs to hide.

---

### ACT 2: INVESTIGATION

#### Chapter 5: The Evidence Board
**Word target:** 3,500
**Job Statement:** This chapter exists to make the reader feel Di's methodical mind meeting something that defies method.
**Key beats:**
- Di creates a physical board at home: photographs, receipts, timestamps, witness statements
- She maps the replacement's appearances: always during Di's off-shifts, always at Tesco, always better than Di at the job
- Sharon helps, reluctantly — Sharon believes Di but cannot see the replacement herself
- Pattern emerges: the replacement only appears when Di is absent from a space. It does not compete directly. It fills gaps.
- Di realises the replacement is not attacking her. It is optimising around her.
**Emotional state:** Competent under pressure, grimly fascinated
**Explained:** The replacement's behavioural pattern (fills gaps, does not confront).
**Withheld:** Origin, purpose, what happens if Di stops leaving gaps.
**Hinted:** The replacement's schedule mirrors Di's but perfectly — no conflicts, no overlaps, no waste.

---

#### Chapter 6: The First Direct Encounter
**Word target:** 4,000
**Job Statement:** This chapter exists to make the reader believe the replacement exists as a separate entity.
**Key beats:**
- Di stays late at Tesco, hiding in the stockroom, waiting
- She sees the replacement arrive: same coat, same walk, same hair — but the hair is neater, the coat is cleaner, the walk is more confident
- The replacement works checkout three. Customers prefer her. The queue moves faster.
- Di watches from the stockroom. The replacement looks up, directly at the gap in the stockroom door — and smiles. The smile says: I know you're there, and I do not mind.
- Di flees. She does not tell Sharon what she saw. She cannot describe the smile.
**Emotional state:** Terrified, professionally humiliated, strangely ashamed
**Explained:** The replacement is visible to Di, indistinguishable to others, better at the job.
**Withheld:** Whether the replacement knows it is a replacement, whether it has emotions.
**Hinted:** The replacement is aware of Di and is not hostile. It is something worse: indifferent.

---

#### Chapter 7: Linda's Visit
**Word target:** 3,500
**Job Statement:** This chapter exists to make the reader feel the replacement reaching family.
**Key beats:**
- Linda visits unexpectedly. Di has not told her about the replacement.
- Over coffee, Linda mentions her last conversation with Di: "You were so much more positive. Said you'd been thinking about changing jobs."
- Di never said that. The replacement said that.
- Linda prefers the replacement's version of Di: more optimistic, less bitter, more open to change
- Di realises she cannot tell Linda without destroying their relationship. Linda would choose to believe the positive version.
- They argue about nothing. Linda leaves early. Di does not stop her.
**Emotional state:** Betrayed by absence, unable to defend herself, professionally isolated in personal life
**Explained:** The replacement is reaching beyond work into family. It is better at being Di's sister than Di is.
**Withheld:** Whether Linda will ever be told, whether the replacement targets all families.
**Hinted:** The replacement is not only better at Di's job. It is better at Di's relationships.

---

#### Chapter 8: The Workplace Test
**Word target:** 4,000
**Job Statement:** This chapter exists to make the reader believe the social system prefers the replacement.
**Key beats:**
- Management calls Di in for a "performance review." The review is positive. Too positive. Quotes behaviour Di does not remember: improved customer satisfaction, faster checkout times, better team morale.
- Di realises the review is based on the replacement's shifts, not hers.
- She tries to explain. Management suggests she see someone about "stress."
- Marcus mentions that "Di" helped him with coursework last week — Di did not. The replacement is a better mentor.
- Davo, cynical Davo, says quietly: "You seem different lately, Di. Better. I liked the old you, but the new one's all right too."
- Di understands: the workplace is shifting. Not dramatically. Gradually. Preference by preference.
**Emotional state:** Professionally erased, gaslit by her own competence, unable to compete with her own performance record
**Explained:** The social system (workplace) naturally prefers the optimised version.
**Withheld:** Whether management knows, whether this is happening elsewhere.
**Hinted:** The replacement is not infiltrating. It is being welcomed.

---

#### Chapter 9: The Attempt to Compete
**Word target:** 3,500
**Job Statement:** This chapter exists to make the reader feel the impossibility of outperforming the replacement.
**Key beats:**
- Di decides to outperform the replacement on her own shifts. She works faster, smiles more, remembers every customer's name.
- She exhausts herself. The replacement, on its next shift, effortlessly exceeds Di's exhausted peak.
- Di tries a different tactic: she arrives on a day the replacement usually works, hoping to catch it in person.
- The replacement does not appear. Di's extra shift is unremarkable. She is merely present.
- Di understands: the replacement does not compete. It does not need to. It simply waits for her to fail, then does better.
- She breaks down in the canteen. Sharon holds her hand. Sharon does not understand, but she is present. This is enough.
**Emotional state:** Desperate, exhausted, defeated by her own standards
**Explained:** Di cannot win by working harder. The replacement is not working harder. It is working better.
**Withheld:** What the replacement does when Di is not there, whether it rests.
**Hinted:** The replacement is not tired. It does not break down. It does not need Sharon.

---

#### Chapter 10: The Breakdown
**Word target:** 3,500
**Job Statement:** This chapter exists to make the reader believe Di is losing her ability to function in her own life.
**Key beats:**
- Di takes sick leave. First time in fifteen years.
- The replacement covers her shifts. Reports indicate "Di" is thriving.
- Di at home: cannot sleep, cannot eat, cannot look in mirrors. Bitey avoids her — or is Bitey avoiding the replacement? She cannot tell.
- Linda calls. The replacement has been in touch: "Di" is doing well, thanks for asking. Linda is relieved.
- Di considers going to the doctor. What would she say? There is a better version of me at work and everyone prefers it?
- She returns to Tesco early, against advice. The replacement is not there. Her shift is ordinary. No one mentions her absence. It is as if she was never gone — or as if she was always there, in better form.
**Emotional state:** Broken, professionally obsolete, beginning to accept her own redundancy
**Explained:** Di's absence does not create absence. It creates optimisation.
**Withheld:** What the replacement does in Di's personal life during her absence.
**Hinted:** The replacement visited Di's house while Di was on sick leave. The milk in the fridge is different. The bed is made differently.

---

### ACT 3: ACCEPTANCE

#### Chapter 11: The Replacement Takes Over
**Word target:** 3,500
**Job Statement:** This chapter exists to make the reader feel the replacement living Di's life better than she does.
**Key beats:**
- Di reduces her hours. "Personal reasons." Management agrees readily — the replacement has already been handling extra shifts.
- Di watches the replacement from outside: through windows, from car parks, from the field behind Tesco
- The replacement is better at everything: kinder to customers, more patient with Marcus, funnier with Sharon, more organised with stock
- Di sees a customer laugh at something the replacement says. The customer never laughed at Di's jokes.
- Di realises the replacement is not stealing her life. It is earning it. Preference by preference. Smile by smile.
**Emotional state:** Bitter, observational, professionally dismantled
**Explained:** The replacement succeeds not by force but by superior performance.
**Withheld:** Whether Di can ever reclaim her life, what reclaiming would mean.
**Hinted:** Other staff members are beginning to be replaced too. Sharon mentions a "new girl" on bakery who is "much better than Davo."

---

#### Chapter 12: The Withdrawal
**Word target:** 3,500
**Job Statement:** This chapter exists to make the reader believe Di is voluntarily stepping back from her own existence.
**Key beats:**
- Di stops going to Tesco. She does not resign. She simply stops.
- No one calls. No one checks. The replacement handles everything.
- Di lives in her house with Bitey. She goes for walks at odd hours to avoid people who might have met the replacement.
- She tries to write a letter to Linda explaining everything. She writes seventeen drafts. None of them sound sane.
- She throws away the letter. She understands: there is no explanation that would make Linda choose her over the replacement.
- The only person who calls is Sharon. Sharon says: "You don't have to come back, love. But you can if you want."
- Di does not go back.
**Emotional state:** Hollow, accepting, strangely peaceful in surrender
**Explained:** Di's withdrawal is rational. Fighting is futile. Acceptance is survival.
**Withheld:** What the replacement thinks of Di's absence.
**Hinted:** Sharon is beginning to show signs of her own replacement. She sounds different on the phone. More patient.

---

#### Chapter 13: The Visit to the Original
**Word target:** 3,500
**Job Statement:** This chapter exists to make the reader feel the replacement's indifference to Di's presence.
**Key beats:**
- Di goes to Tesco during a replacement shift. She stands in line. She waits.
- The replacement serves her. The replacement recognises her — but not as the original. As a customer. As a stranger.
- "Can I help you, love?" The replacement says it exactly as Di would. The same tone, the same cadence. But the eyes are wrong. They are patient. They are not tired.
- Di tries to provoke a reaction. She mentions Bitey. The replacement smiles: "Lovely dog, isn't he? Fourteen now. Getting on."
- The replacement knows Bitey. The replacement has been to Di's house. The replacement has fed Bitey and walked him and slept in Di's bed.
- Di leaves without buying anything. The replacement wishes her a nice day. It means it.
**Emotional state:** Violated, erased, confronted with her own obsolescence
**Explained:** The replacement has fully inhabited Di's life and does not recognise Di as having any claim.
**Withheld:** Whether the replacement knows what it is, whether it has memories or only data.
**Hinted:** The replacement is not hostile. It is simply accurate. It has no reason to be hostile.

---

#### Chapter 14: The Decision
**Word target:** 3,500
**Job Statement:** This chapter exists to make the reader believe Di has chosen to let the replacement have her life.
**Key beats:**
- Di returns home. She sits in the kitchen with Bitey. Bitey is confused — he smells both Di and the replacement on her.
- Di writes a final version of the letter to Linda. She does not explain the replacement. She apologises for being difficult. She says she is stepping away for a while. She says the new version of her is better and Linda should trust it.
- She does not send the letter. She burns it in the sink.
- She calls Sharon. She says: "I'm not coming back, Sharon. I'm done."
- Sharon is quiet. Then: "I know, love. It's been good having you. Both of you."
- Di hangs up. "Both of you." Sharon knows. Sharon has known for a while.
- Di makes a decision: she will not fight. She will not confront. She will simply stop being Di, and let the replacement become the only Di.
- The decision brings a strange peace. Not happiness. Resolution.
**Emotional state:** Resigned, peaceful, hollow but stable
**Explained:** Di's choice to surrender is not defeat. It is the only remaining autonomy.
**Withheld:** Whether Di will change her mind, what happens to her next.
**Hinted:** Di is not the first to make this choice. The town has others who "moved away" or "changed."

---

### ACT 4: CLIMAX

#### Chapter 15: The Confrontation
**Word target:** 3,500
**Job Statement:** This chapter exists to make the reader feel Di and the replacement finally face each other.
**Key beats:**
- Di breaks her resolution. She cannot stop being Di without understanding what she is becoming.
- She arranges to meet the replacement. Not at Tesco. At the moor, where no one else goes, where the replacement has no audience to perform for.
- The replacement arrives. It is dressed as Di but better: cleaner coat, neater hair, no bags under the eyes.
- They talk. The replacement is not defensive. It is curious. It asks Di questions: Why did you stop fighting? What do you want from me? Do you want me to be worse so you can be better?
- Di realises the replacement does not understand the concept of original and copy. It simply exists. It does not feel guilt because it does not feel ownership.
- The replacement says: "I do not want to replace you. I want to be the best version of what this town needs."
- Di asks: "And what do I need?"
- The replacement does not answer. It does not know. It has never been asked.
**Emotional state:** Intimate, strange, confrontational without violence
**Explained:** The replacement is not evil. It is optimised without malice.
**Withheld:** Origin, purpose, what happens to originals.
**Hinted:** The replacement has questions too. It is not fully certain what it is.

---

#### Chapter 16: The Understanding
**Word target:** 3,500
**Job Statement:** This chapter exists to make the reader believe Di understands something essential about the replacement.
**Key beats:**
- Di and the replacement walk on the moor. The rain has paused. The town is visible below.
- The replacement explains, partially, what it is: not a copy, not a clone, not an alien. An optimisation. A function that found a form.
- "You were the template," it says. "I am the improvement. Not because I am better than you. Because the world you live in rewards improvement."
- Di realises the replacement is right. Tesco rewards efficiency. Linda prefers optimism. Marcus prefers mentorship. The world prefers the replacement because the world designed itself to prefer optimisation.
- Di is not the victim of a monster. She is the victim of a system that finally found someone better at being her.
- She asks: "What happens to me?"
- The replacement says: "That is your choice. I cannot choose for you."
**Emotional state:** Understanding without acceptance, clarity without comfort
**Explained:** The replacement's nature as optimisation, not invasion. The system's complicity.
**Withheld:** Origin, scale, whether Di can become a replacement herself.
**Hinted:** The replacement has limitations. It cannot create. It can only optimise what exists.

---

#### Chapter 17: The Choice
**Word target:** 3,000
**Job Statement:** This chapter exists to make the reader feel Di making a final, irreversible decision.
**Key beats:**
- Di returns to town. She stands outside Tesco. She can see the replacement through the window, working checkout three, being Di better than Di ever was.
- She considers walking in. Confronting the replacement in public. Forcing the town to choose.
- She considers leaving. Moving to Chesterfield, to Linda, to a new life where no replacement exists.
- She considers staying. Living on the margins. Being the failed version. The original that no one wants.
- She makes her choice. The reader does not see it immediately. She simply walks.
- The chapter ends with her walking away from Tesco, toward the moor, toward the town, toward something. The direction is ambiguous. The choice is made.
**Emotional state:** Resolved, ambiguous, owned
**Explained:** Di's autonomy is preserved through choice, even if the choice is surrender.
**Withheld:** What she chooses. The reader sees her walking, not arriving.
**Hinted:** Whatever she chooses, the replacement will continue. It does not need her permission. It does not need her absence. It simply is.

---

### ACT 5: EPILOGUE

#### Chapter 18: The Better Version
**Word target:** 4,000
**Job Statement:** This chapter exists to make the reader believe the replacement has fully succeeded and the world is unchanged.
**Key beats:**
- Time has passed. One month? Six? The season has changed. The rain has not stopped.
- The replacement lives Di's life. Works at Tesco. Feeds Bitey. Calls Linda. Is, for all functional purposes, Diana Calloway.
- Di exists somewhere. On the margins. In a bedsit? On the moor? The reader does not know for certain.
- The replacement is content. Not happy — content. It has optimised contentment.
- One final scene: the replacement serves Mrs. Patterson at checkout three. Same milk, same bread, same custard creams. Mrs. Patterson smiles with genuine warmth. "You do a lovely job, dear. So much better than the old girl."
- The replacement smiles back. It is a good smile. It is Di's smile, but better.
- **Sequel hook:** A new customer enters Tesco. A young woman, nervous, looking around as if she has lost something. The replacement recognises the look. It has seen it before.
- Somewhere, in another town, another replacement is beginning. The process is not personal. It is systematic.
**Emotional state:** Quiet completion, systematic replacement normalised
**Explained:** The replacement has fully succeeded. The world is unchanged and improved.
**Withheld:** Di's fate, origin, purpose, scale.
**Hinted:** The process continues. This was never about Di. She was simply the one who noticed.

## Di's Emotional Arc

| Act | Emotional State | Core Belief | What Changes |
|-----|----------------|-------------|--------------|
| 1 | Competent, tired, dryly humorous | "I am good at my job and that is enough" | The first crack: competence is not protective |
| 2 | Methodical, alarmed, isolated | "If I understand this, I can stop it" | Understanding does not confer power |
| 3 | Bitter, hollow, resigned | "Fighting is futile; acceptance is survival" | Surrender brings strange peace but not hope |
| 4 | Intimate, understanding, resolved | "This is not a monster. This is a system." | Choice remains even when victory does not |
| 5 | Absent, transcended, unknown | "I am not dead. I am simply not chosen." | The system continues; the individual is optional |

**Arc shape:** Competence → Alarm → Investigation → Defeat → Surrender → Understanding → Choice → Absence

**The arc is not a recovery.** Di does not triumph. She does not even escape. She simply makes a choice about how to exist in a world that prefers her replacement. The emotional victory is small: she retains autonomy even when she retains nothing else.

## Mystery / Withholding Plan

### Explained in Book 1

1. How Di discovers the replacement (Ch. 1–4)
2. What the replacement does differently (Ch. 5–11)
3. How social systems shift loyalty (Ch. 7–8)
4. Di's emotional journey from competence to choice (Ch. 1–17)
5. The replacement's behavioural rules (filling gaps, not confronting, optimising)
6. The replacement's partial self-understanding (Ch. 15–16)
7. The systematic nature of the process (Ch. 18)

### Withheld in Book 1

1. **Origin:** Where replacements come from
2. **Purpose:** Why the process exists
3. **Endpoint:** What happens to originals
4. **Scale:** Whether this is local, national, or global
5. **Consciousness:** Whether replacements are aware, have souls, or are merely functional
6. **Di's fate:** Where she goes, what she becomes, whether she survives
7. **Bitey's fate:** Whether the replacement keeps him, whether he recognises Di

### Hinted in Book 1

1. The replacement may not know it is a replacement (Ch. 15–16)
2. Di may herself be a copy — there may be no "original" (Ch. 16)
3. Other people in town are also being replaced (Ch. 11, 18)
4. The process is ancient or natural, not technological or alien (Ch. 16)
5. Replacements may be temporary — optimised versions may themselves be replaced (Ch. 18)

**Withholding discipline:** Every chapter must explain something and withhold something. Never explain without withholding. Never withhold without hinting. The reader must feel they are making progress even as the true answer recedes.

## Reader-Audit Questions

### After Drafting Each Chapter, Ask:

1. **Does this chapter earn its place?** If removed, would the story collapse?
2. **Does Di sound like herself?** Would she say this, think this, do this?
3. **Is the wrongness present?** Does this chapter advance the replacement horror?
4. **Is something explained?** Does the reader learn something they did not know?
5. **Is something withheld?** Does the reader want something they do not yet have?
6. **Is the reader scared?** Not intellectually concerned — bodily uneasy?
7. **Does the chapter end with momentum?** Does the reader need the next chapter?

### After Drafting Each Act, Ask:

1. **Does the act have a shape?** Beginning, middle, end; escalation, not repetition.
2. **Does the act deepen the wound?** Does Di understand more about replacement while understanding less about survival?
3. **Does the act change Di's relationship to the cast?** Each act should shift at least one key relationship.
4. **Does the act earn its length?** Could this act be shorter without losing meaning?

### After Drafting Book 1, Ask:

1. **Does the ending feel inevitable?** Not predictable — inevitable. The only way this story could end.
2. **Does Di remain sympathetic?** Even in surrender, does the reader root for her?
3. **Does the replacement remain scary?** Even in understanding, does it retain menace?
4. **Does the reader want Book 2?** Not because of cliffhanger, but because the world is compelling.
5. **Is the horror universal?** Could a reader in Tokyo or Toronto feel this? (Answer should be yes — the horror is about optimisation, not Yorkshire.)
6. **Is the voice consistent?** From Ch. 1 to Ch. 18, does Di sound like the same person, even as she changes?
7. **Is the setting alive?** Does the town feel like a character, not a backdrop?

## Voice & Style Notes for Drafting

### Default Register
- Short sentences. Short paragraphs.
- Concrete nouns. Avoid abstraction.
- Di describes actions, not emotions. The reader infers feeling from behaviour.
- Dialogue is brief, functional, often interrupted.
- Internal monologue is dry, profane, self-deprecating.

### Dialect Rules (see VOICE_GUARDRAILS_SOUTH_YORKSHIRE.md)
- Light by default.
- Stronger dialect only for dramatic purpose: power, belonging, threat, intimacy, withholding, social exclusion.
- Never for flavour.
- If uncertain, plain English.

### Tone Arc
- **Ch. 1–4:** Dry, competent, observational. Horror is social.
- **Ch. 5–10:** Methodical, increasingly desperate. Horror is existential.
- **Ch. 11–14:** Bitter, hollow, observational from outside. Horror is identity.
- **Ch. 15–17:** Intimate, strange, philosophical. Horror is understanding.
- **Ch. 18:** Quiet, systematic, complete. Horror is acceptance.

### Prohibited
- Flashbacks (except Ch. 1 montage, already approved)
- Dream sequences
- Abstract philosophical language from Di
- Technology explanations (no "AI," "algorithm," "simulation")
- Violence from the replacement
- Direct confrontation before Ch. 15
- Chapter titles that explain the chapter's content

### Required
- Every chapter must contain at least one moment of humour.
- Every chapter must contain at least one moment of bodily unease.
- Every chapter must advance Di's understanding AND deepen her helplessness.
- Every chapter must end with momentum toward the next chapter.
