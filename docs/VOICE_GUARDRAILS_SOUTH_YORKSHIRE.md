# Voice Guardrails — South Yorkshire Setting
## The Better Version series

**Date:** 2026-07-16
**Status:** Active — applies to all drafting, revision, and editing
**Profile:** horror-series
**Applies to:** Diana "Di" Calloway narrative voice, dialogue, internal monologue, and any POV character in this series

---

## The Golden Rule

> Voice is not costume. Voice is consequence.

Di Calloway sounds like she does because of where she was born, where she works, who she left, and what she has endured. She does not sound like this for literary effect. Every word she uses — including every word she does *not* use — must be explicable by her life, not by the author's desire for regional colour.

---

## Eight Operational Rules

### 1. Regional markers are light, not loud

Dialect markers must appear sparingly. A few words per chapter at most — never every paragraph. If a reader starts noticing the dialect, it has become costume.

**Acceptable:**
> "You sound tired, love."
> "I reckon she's got nowt better to do."
> "Summat's off with that delivery."

**Unacceptable:**
> "Ee bah gum, it's reet proper mardy out there, love, and I'm not chuffed about it, like."

The test: if a sentence reads like someone doing an impression of the North, rewrite it in plain English.

### 2. Class texture comes through life, not vocabulary

The reader should know Di is working-class because of:
- What she does (shift supervisor at Tesco, fifteen years)
- What she notices (price of cheese, who is drinking again, which supplier is late)
- What she owns (rented terrace, old dog, nothing new)
- What she resents (management initiatives, people who "moved on")
- What she laughs at (the absurdity of her own situation)
- What exhausts her (the canteen, the rain, the customers, the nothing-new)

Not because she says "summat" instead of "something."

### 3. Avoid stage-Northern caricature

**Never use:**
- "Ee by gum," "ecky thump," "now then," "ey up" as punctuation
- Excessive dropping of definite articles ("going t'shop" — occasional is fine; constant is costume)
- Phonetic spelling ("wot" for "what," "fook" for "fuck")
- Broad vowel transcription in narrative prose ("booook" for "book")
- Coronation Street cadence — the musical upswing at the end of sentences, the performative warmth
- "Oop North" generalisations that apply to anywhere from Sheffield to Aberdeen

### 4. Dialect is not decoration

Do not reach for a regional word because the prose feels too generic. If plain English says it better, use plain English. The setting is already doing work (rain, terraces, closed pit, Tesco). The voice does not need to carry the entire regional burden.

**Wrong:**
> The rain came down proper mardy that morning, all grey and spiteful-like.

**Right:**
> The rain had been there since October. She couldn't remember what dry felt like.

### 5. Unfamiliar terms must be self-explanatory from context

If a regional word is used, the surrounding sentence must make the meaning obvious without footnotes or glossaries. No reader should have to stop and look anything up.

**Acceptable:**
> "You reet?" Sharon asked. — Context: greeting, concern. Meaning is obvious.
> "She were proper mardy about it." — Context: describing someone's mood. "Mardy" = sulky/grumpy. Clear from usage.

**Unacceptable:**
> "She were proper mardy, like a mardy bum, all mardy in the canteen." — Repeating the word without context clues. If you need to explain it, don't use it.

### 6. Profanity is punctuation, not emphasis

Di swears because working-class Northern English uses profanity as grammatical seasoning — not to shock, not to prove toughness. "Fuck" appears in internal monologue, not in front of customers. "Shit" is disappointment, not alarm. "Bastard" is affectionate as often as hostile.

**Do not:**
- Front-load profanity to establish edginess
- Use profanity where plain language is more precise
- Make profanity the only marker of working-class identity

**Do:**
- Vary the weight of profanity with emotional intensity
- Let silence carry what swearing cannot
- Use "Christ" and "Jesus" as often as "fuck" — Northern working-class blasphemy is its own register

### 7. When uncertain, prefer plain grounded English

If you hesitate over whether a word or construction is authentic, use the simpler version. Authenticity through restraint is better than authenticity through excess.

> "I don't know what you're on about" > "I dun't know what tha're on about"
> "She was angry" > "She were reet vexed"

The reader will trust a restrained voice more than a flamboyant one. A restrained voice that misses by a fraction is still credible. A flamboyant voice that misses by a fraction is a caricature.

### 8. Voice must survive the cosmic scale

Di's voice must remain consistent from Book 1 (personal) to Book 5 (cosmic). She processes existential horror through the lens of a shift supervisor. She does not become philosophical as the scale increases. She becomes quieter.

**Book 1:**
> "Fucking hell. There's two of me."

**Book 5 (same voice, larger scale):**
> "So you're telling me there's a pool of versions of me, and I'm not the best one? Well, fuck."

Not:
> "The infinite recursion of identity suggests that the self is merely a provisional instantiation of a deeper ontological process."

If Di ever uses words like "ontological" or "recursion" unironically, flag immediately. She should describe cosmic concepts in concrete, profane, working-class terms. The voice is South Yorkshire: dry, rhythmic, allergic to pretension. 'Sound,' 'reckon,' 'summat,' 'nowt' — these are not affectations. They are her native grammar.

## Prohibited Words and Constructions

| Prohibited | Why | Replacement |
|------------|-----|-------------|
| "Ee by gum" / "Ecky thump" / "Now then" (as verbal filler) | Stage-Northern cliché | Silence, profanity, or plain English |
| Phonetic spelling ("wot," "fook," "booook") | Treats dialect as novelty | Standard spelling; let syntax do the work |
| Excessive dropped articles ("going t'pub" every sentence) | Costume effect | Occasional, in dialogue only; standard articles in narrative |
| "Oop North" / "Northern folk" / "us Yorkshire lot" (in narrative) | Authorial generalisation, not character voice | Specific description of place and behaviour |
| "Mardy" used more than once per chapter | Becomes tic | Vary: sulky, off, not right, in a mood |
| "Proper" used more than twice per chapter | Becomes tic | Vary: really, actually, straight-up, plain |
| Any word the author looked up to "sound Northern" | Inauthentic by definition | Plain English |

## The Confidence Test

Before finalising any sentence in Di's voice, ask:

1. Would she say this, or am I making her say this?
2. Does this word belong to her life, or to my idea of her region?
3. If I removed all regional markers, would the sentence still work?
4. Is the emotion coming through, or is the dialect getting in the way?
5. Would a South Yorkshire reader recognise this as real, or as an outsider's impression?

If the answer to (5) is uncertain, rewrite in plain English.
